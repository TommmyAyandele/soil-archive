## Who Were the Ogoni Nine?

On 10 November 1995, nine Ogoni men were hanged by the Nigerian military government of Sani Abacha. Their names are:

**Ken Saro-Wiwa**, **Saturday Dobee**, **Nordu Eawo**, **Daniel Gbooko**, **Paul Levura**, **Felix Nuate**, **Baribor Bera**, **Barinem Kiobel**, and **John Kpuinen**.

They were accused of the 1994 murder of four Ogoni chiefs — charges that Amnesty International, the United Nations Human Rights Commission, and the Bar Human Rights Committee of England and Wales documented as falling below internationally recognised standards of fair trial. They were tried before a military-appointed tribunal that denied them the basic protections of due process. Defence counsel Gani Fawehinmi and Oronto Douglas were on multiple occasions denied access to their clients and obstructed from presenting their case before the tribunal. Amnesty International, the United Nations Human Rights Commission, the Commonwealth Heads of Government, and the governments of the United States, United Kingdom, and South Africa formally called for a stay of execution. The executions were carried out on the morning of 10 November 1995.

## The Context: Shell and the Destruction of Ogoniland

The Ogoni people occupy approximately 1,000 square kilometres of the Niger Delta in south-eastern Nigeria. Beginning in 1958, Shell Petroleum Development Company — operating under licence with the Nigerian government — extracted oil from Ogoniland for decades. The result was environmental devastation on a scale that the United Nations Environment Programme, in a landmark 2011 report, described as one of the world's most severe cases of hydrocarbon pollution. Farmland was destroyed. Fishing grounds were poisoned. Drinking water was contaminated. The Ogoni people received almost nothing of the oil wealth extracted from beneath their land.

Ken Saro-Wiwa, author of the novel *Sozaboy* and creator of the television series *Basi and Company*, redirected his public platform toward Ogoni rights activism in the late 1980s. In 1990, he co-founded the Movement for the Survival of the Ogoni People — MOSOP — and drafted the Ogoni Bill of Rights, which demanded political autonomy, environmental reparations, and a share of oil revenues for the Ogoni people.

## MOSOP and the Ogoni Bill of Rights

MOSOP organised one of the largest peaceful mass movements in Nigerian history. On 4 January 1993, an estimated 300,000 Ogoni people — roughly half the entire population — marched in what became known as Ogoni Day, demanding an end to Shell's operations and recognition of Ogoni rights. Shell subsequently withdrew its workers from Ogoniland, though the damage to the environment remained.

The Nigerian government, whose revenues depended on oil extraction, viewed MOSOP's success with alarm. Beginning in 1993, the military government of Ibrahim Babangida and then Sani Abacha deployed the Rivers State Internal Security Task Force to Ogoniland. The Task Force, under Lt Col Paul Okuntimo, carried out systematic human rights abuses against Ogoni communities — raids, beatings, killings, and arrests.

## The 1995 Tribunal and Execution

In May 1994, four prominent Ogoni chiefs who had been critics of MOSOP were killed at a rally in Giokoo. Ken Saro-Wiwa was not present. He was nonetheless arrested, along with eight other MOSOP activists, and charged with incitement to murder.

The tribunal that tried them was appointed by the military government. It operated without the procedural protections of civilian courts. Defence counsel were repeatedly obstructed. A key prosecution witness later recanted, stating that he had been bribed to testify. International observers who attended the proceedings raised serious concerns about the fairness of the process.

Ken Saro-Wiwa had prepared a formal statement for delivery to the tribunal. He was not permitted to read it. The text of that statement was subsequently published by his family and forms part of this collection.

On 10 November 1995, all nine men were executed by hanging at Port Harcourt Prison. The executions provoked international outcry. Nigeria was suspended from the Commonwealth of Nations. The United States and European Union imposed limited sanctions.

## The Posthumous Pardon

In 2024, Nigerian President Bola Ahmed Tinubu granted posthumous pardons to Ken Saro-Wiwa and the other eight men. The pardon was welcomed by Saro-Wiwa's family and supporters as a step toward acknowledgement of the injustice, though many advocates have continued to call for full exoneration — a formal legal finding of innocence — and concrete reparative action for the communities of Ogoniland.

As of 2024, environmental remediation of Ogoniland remains incomplete. The Hydrocarbon Pollution Remediation Project launched in 2016 has faced sustained criticism from Ogoni community representatives and independent environmental monitors regarding the pace and adequacy of clean-up operations.
