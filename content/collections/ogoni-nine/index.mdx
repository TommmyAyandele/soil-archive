## Who Were the Ogoni Nine?

The Ogoni Nine were nine vocal and resilient indigenous leaders — **Ken Saro-Wiwa**, **Barinem Kiobel**, **John Kpuinen**, **Baribor Bera**, **Felix Nuate**, **Paul Levula**, **Saturday Dobee**, **Nordu Eawo**, and **Daniel Gbokoo** — who criticised the inhumane environmental damage and destructive extractive operations in the Niger Delta region, led by foreign oil companies, particularly the Royal Dutch Shell Company.

As part of their non-violent activism, they formed an environmental justice organisation known as the Movement for the Survival of the Ogoni People (MOSOP), which drew attention to the devastating impacts of oil extraction on the plight and livelihoods of Ogoniland, a predominantly fishing community. The organisation created the Ogoni Bill of Rights in August 1990, a historic document that articulated the grievances, sufferings, and demands of the Ogoni people for resource control, political autonomy, and a fair distribution of resource wealth.

## MOSOP and the Ogoni Bill of Rights

After failing to secure compensation for the Ogoni people, MOSOP led a series of protests against the collusion between the Nigerian military government of Sani Abacha and multinational oil firms. Notable among these was the Ogoni Day March — on 4 January 1993, approximately 300,000 Ogoni people organised a mass demonstration against Shell's activities and demanded that the company vacate Ogoniland. The action forced Shell to withdraw its employees from the area, though the environmental destruction it left behind remained.

## The 1995 Tribunal and Execution

The Ogoni Nine were arrested in 1994 on charges of alleged complicity in the deaths of four Ogoni chiefs at a political rally in Giokoo. Their trial before a special military tribunal has since been described as a sham that fell below international standards for a fair trial — and widely understood as punishment for their criticism of the Abacha-led administration.

The tribunal denied the activists access to counsel and the opportunity to appeal its judgment. Ken Saro-Wiwa had prepared a speech for delivery before the tribunal but was not permitted to read it. The creation of that speech forms a central part of this public humanities project, which aims to spotlight a suppressed personal testimony that might otherwise be lost to history.

The Ogoni Nine were hanged on 10 November 1995 at Port Harcourt Prison. Their execution sparked global outrage and led to the suspension of Nigeria from the Commonwealth of Nations.

## The Censored Speech

Before their execution, Ken Saro-Wiwa — writer, MOSOP leader, and the most prominent of the nine — had prepared a six-minute speech he intended to read before the special military tribunal. The military government of Sani Abacha prevented him from delivering it.

An excerpt from the transcript of this speech is available on Wikisource, an online digital library. Since 1995, activists and civil society organisations have staged posthumous readings of the speech on various occasions. In 2020, a Nigeria-based reading group known as The Uyo Book Club hosted a special reading to mark the 25th anniversary of Saro-Wiwa's death, featuring excerpts from the censored statement.

## The Posthumous Pardon and Ongoing Demands

Thirty years later, Nigeria's President Bola Tinubu granted a posthumous presidential pardon and national honours to the Ogoni Nine — a decision met with mixed reactions among family members and those from the Niger Delta.

For many, while the pardon is a welcome development, it implies that the nine men committed a crime when in reality they did not. Critics argue that their executions were a cover for the Abacha administration's collusion in the destruction of Ogoniland, and that a pardon is not enough. They have called for full exoneration — a formal legal finding of wrongful conviction — as well as concrete environmental remediation of Ogoniland, a demand the Nigerian government has yet to fulfil.
